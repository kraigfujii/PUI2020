An Introduction To:

Photogrammetry

Welcome! This is a short guide on photogrammetry: what it is, the process, and how it can be used.

  • Introduction

    Photogrammetry is the process of creating realistic digital models using multiple photos of real-world objects and artifacts. There are many different uses for photogrammetry that spans multiple industries, including film, games, education, and research.

    As of 2020, the photogrammetry process is the closet we have come to digitally capturing the world around us. While photogrammetry as a field has existed for decades, the advent of modern computing has allowed companies and individuals to create photorealisttic copies of environments and objects. These models can be shared nearly in any digital setting or device – even on social media!

  • Use Cases

    Today, photogrammetry is used in a wide range of applications. From film, games, museums, or education, there are countless opportunities to integrate this process that brings value.

    Education

    Photogrammetry produces the most accurate and realistic reproduction of physical objects that we have available. Creating models of historical objects or artifacts allows educational institutes to research and share resources virtually.

    Museums

    Photogrammetry assets can easily be implemented for museums of all sizes, either integrated into exhibitions or as an online showcases.

    Entertainment

    The photogrammetry process has been increasingly common in both the film amd games industries, as virtual sets and photo-real art assets.

  • Capture Process

    The very first step of the photogrammetry capture process is to take photos! When conducting a capture, the goal is to take photos all around your chosen object from different angles – the interpolation of these images is what's required to make a realistic 3D object. While the exactly number of photos depends on the object, most high-quality photogrammetry captures consist of at least three rotations of 36 images at different heights – roughly 100 photos at the minimum.

    This process can be done on hardware accessible to most photographers: a modern, SLR-camera with a fixed-lens is recommended. Having specialized equipment, such as a shutter-released tripods or ring flash for flat lighting is recommended but not required, though it may result in extended processing time.

  • Processing & Optimization

    Once the capture process has been completed, the next step is to compile all the photos and use specialized software, which will automatically create a base 3D mesh. This normally requires a high-powered computer with a large amount of memory.

    By default, this model will be extremely large and contains too much detail to be useful for most applications. The model is then manually retopologized, which may take several days and additional 3D software.
